## 1. Verbatim criteria — §13.1 (every criterion, sub-item, note, quoted exactly, with page number)

> **Source of all quotes below:** ICHD-3 (2018), *Cephalalgia* 38(1), © International Headache Society 2018. Journal page numbers = PDF sheet numbers (they coincide in this document).

### §13.1 Pain attributed to a lesion or disease of the trigeminal nerve (parent heading, p. 166)

Heading only; no diagnostic criteria at this level.

### §13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia (p. 166)

**Description (p. 166):** "A disorder characterized by recurrent unilateral brief electric shock-like pains, abrupt in onset and termination, limited to the distribution of one or more divisions of the trigeminal nerve and triggered by innocuous stimuli. It may develop without apparent cause or be a result of another diagnosed disorder. Additionally, there may be concomitant continuous pain of moderate intensity within the distribution(s) of the affected nerve division(s)."

**Previously used terms (p. 166):** "Tic douloureux, primary trigeminal neuralgia."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 166):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain in the distribution(s) of one or more divisions of the trigeminal nerve, with no radiation beyond,¹ and fulfilling criteria B and C
> **B.** Pain has all of the following characteristics:
>  1. lasting from a fraction of a second to two minutes²
>  2. severe intensity³
>  3. electric shock-like, shooting, stabbing or sharp in quality
> **C.** Precipitated by innocuous stimuli within the affected trigeminal distribution⁴
> **D.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis.

**Notes (p. 166):**

> 1. In a few patients, pain may radiate to another division, but it remains within the trigeminal dermatomes.
> 2. Duration can change over time, with paroxysms becoming more prolonged. A minority of patients will report attacks predominantly lasting for >2 minutes.
> 3. Pain may become more severe over time.
> 4. Some attacks may be, or appear to be, spontaneous, but there must be a history or finding of pain provoked by innocuous stimuli to meet this criterion. Ideally, the examining clinician should attempt to confirm the history by replicating the triggering phenomenon. However, this may not always be possible because of the patient's refusal, awkward anatomical location of the trigger and/or other factors.

### §13.1.1.1 Classical trigeminal neuralgia (p. 167)

**Description (p. 167):** "Trigeminal neuralgia developing without apparent cause other than neurovascular compression."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 167):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Demonstration on MRI or during surgery of neurovascular compression (not simply contact), with morphological changes¹ in the trigeminal nerve root.

**Note (p. 167):**

> 1. Typically atrophy or displacement.

#### §13.1.1.1.1 Classical trigeminal neuralgia, purely paroxysmal (p. 167)

**Description (p. 167):** "Classical trigeminal neuralgia without persistent background facial pain."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 167):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1.1 Classical trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Pain-free between attacks in the affected trigeminal distribution.

#### §13.1.1.1.2 Classical trigeminal neuralgia with concomitant continuous pain (p. 167)

**Previously used terms (p. 167):** "Atypical trigeminal neuralgia; trigeminal neuralgia type 2."

**Description (p. 167):** "Classical trigeminal neuralgia with persistent background facial pain."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 167):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1.1 Classical trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Concomitant continuous or near-continuous pain between attacks in the affected trigeminal distribution.

### §13.1.1.2 Secondary trigeminal neuralgia (p. 167)

**Description (p. 167):** "Trigeminal neuralgia caused by an underlying disease. Clinical examination shows sensory changes in a significant proportion of these patients."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 167):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia, either purely paroxysmal or associated with concomitant continuous or near-continuous pain
> **B.** An underlying disease has been demonstrated that is known to be able to cause, and explaining, the neuralgia¹
> **C.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis.²

**Notes (p. 167):**

> 1. Recognized causes are tumour in the cerebellopontine angle, arteriovenous malformation and multiple sclerosis.

*(Note ² superscript is present on criterion C at p. 167; the note-2 text is not separately printed in the §13.1.1.2 note block on the page — the printed Note list for §13.1.1.2 contains only item 1. The subtype-specific causes for MS / space-occupying lesion / other are given in the sub-entries below.)*

#### §13.1.1.2.1 Trigeminal neuralgia attributed to multiple sclerosis (p. 168)

**Coded elsewhere (p. 168):** "13.13.1 Central neuropathic pain attributed to multiple sclerosis."

**Description (p. 168):** "Trigeminal neuralgia caused by a multiple sclerosis (MS) plaque or plaques in the pons or trigeminal nerve root entry zone, and associated with other symptoms and/or clinical signs or laboratory findings of MS."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 168):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Both of the following:
>  1. multiple sclerosis (MS) has been diagnosed
>  2. an MS plaque at the trigeminal root entry zone or in the pons affecting the intrapontine primary afferents has been demonstrated by MRI, or its presence is suggested by routine electrophysiological studies¹ showing impairment of the trigeminal pathways
> **C.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis.

**Note (p. 168):**

> 1. Blink reflex or trigeminal evoked potentials.

#### §13.1.1.2.2 Trigeminal neuralgia attributed to space-occupying lesion (p. 168)

**Description (p. 168):** "Trigeminal neuralgia caused by contact between the affected trigeminal nerve and a space-occupying lesion."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 168):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Both of the following:
>  1. a space-occupying lesion in contact with the affected trigeminal nerve has been demonstrated
>  2. pain has developed after identification of the lesion, or led to its discovery
> **C.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis.

#### §13.1.1.2.3 Trigeminal neuralgia attributed to other cause (p. 168)

**Description (p. 168):** "Trigeminal neuralgia caused by an underlying disease other than those described above."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 168):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia, either purely paroxysmal or associated with concomitant continuous or near-continuous pain, but not necessarily unilateral
> **B.** Both of the following:
>  1. a disorder other than those described above, but known to be able to cause trigeminal neuralgia, has been diagnosed¹
>  2. pain has developed after onset of the disorder, or led to its discovery
> **C.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis.

**Note (p. 169):**

> 1. Recognized causes are skull-base bone deformity, connective tissue disease, arteriovenous malformation, dural arteriovenous fistula and genetic causes of neuropathy or nerve hyperexcitability.

### §13.1.1.3 Idiopathic trigeminal neuralgia (p. 169)

**Description (p. 169):** "Trigeminal neuralgia with neither electrophysiological tests nor MRI showing significant abnormalities."

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 169):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia, either purely paroxysmal or associated with concomitant continuous or near-continuous pain
> **B.** Neither 13.1.1.1 Classical trigeminal neuralgia nor 13.1.1.2 Secondary trigeminal neuralgia has been confirmed by adequate investigation including electrophysiological tests and MRI¹
> **C.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis.

**Note (p. 169):**

> 1. A contact between a blood vessel and the trigeminal nerve and/or nerve root is a common finding on neuroimaging in healthy subjects. When such a contact is found in the presence of 13.1.1 Trigeminal neuralgia but without evidence of morphological changes (e.g. atrophy or displacement) in the nerve root, the criteria for 13.1.1.1 Classical trigeminal neuralgia are not fulfilled and the condition is considered idiopathic.

#### §13.1.1.3.1 Idiopathic trigeminal neuralgia, purely paroxysmal (p. 169)

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 169):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1.3 Idiopathic trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Pain-free between attacks in the affected trigeminal distribution.

#### §13.1.1.3.2 Idiopathic trigeminal neuralgia with concomitant continuous pain (p. 169)

**Diagnostic criteria (p. 169):**

> **A.** Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain fulfilling criteria for 13.1.1.3 Idiopathic trigeminal neuralgia
> **B.** Concomitant continuous or near-continuous pain between attacks in the affected trigeminal distribution.

**Scope boundary:** §13.1.2 *Painful trigeminal neuropathy* (post-herpetic, post-traumatic, attributed-to-MS/space-occupying-lesion/other, idiopathic; pp. 169–171) is a **separate parent entity** — continuous burning pain with sensory deficits, *not* the neuralgia (paroxysmal, trigger-provoked) covered by this packet. It is out of scope for a §13.1.1 encoding and should **not** be conflated. Flagged so the writer does not import neuropathy criteria into the neuralgia phenotype.

### 3a. Structural verdict — FLAT additive phenotype is NOT sufficient; needs a two-stage classifier

**This is the load-bearing finding.** §13.1.1 is **not** a flat additive phenotype like §1.1 or §2.2. It is a **two-stage aetiological classifier**:

- **Stage 1 (clinical, chip-driven):** does the patient meet §13.1.1 parent A–D? This *is* encodable as a flat conjunctive phenotype with the existing `Criterion[]` / `role` model. All of A–D are conjunctive (all-of), so all four are `suppress-gate` or a mix of `suppress-gate` (substrate) + `demote-gate` (feature windows). **There is no "≥N of" scoring and no attack-count criterion** — unlike every migraine/TTH/TAC phenotype — so the `scorable` role is essentially unused here; the parent is a pure gate stack.
- **Stage 2 (aetiological, investigation-result-driven):** classical vs secondary vs idiopathic is decided by **MRI / surgical / electrophysiology results**, not by symptom chips. This maps to a `hiddenUntilTrial`-style investigation gate (analogous to the indomethacin gate on §3.2/§3.4), **not** to the additive criterion list.

The current `Phenotype` shape supports **one** `hiddenUntilTrial.gateChip` (a single boolean gate). The TN aetiology sub-tree needs **mutually-exclusive three-way branching on investigation results** (NVC-with-morphological-change → classical; underlying-disease-demonstrated → secondary; neither-on-adequate-MRI+electrophysiology → idiopathic). **The current single-gate `hiddenUntilTrial` cannot express a three-way exclusive branch.**

**→ Recommendation for the writer / system-architect:** encode the **§13.1.1 parent as a flat Class-E phenotype now** (Stage 1 — fully expressible today, no structural change). **Defer the classical/secondary/idiopathic subtype assignment to the subtype-hierarchy layer** (Stage 2 — a genuine structural extension, matching the reference doc's routing note that §13.1/§13.2 "additionally require `system-architect` structural review for the two-stage classifier"). Do **not** attempt to jam three aetiologies into one boolean gate. **This is Class E for the parent + a structural (D-adjacent) architecture decision for the subtype tree.**
