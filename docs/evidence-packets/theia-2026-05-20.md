## Trial identity (verified from PDF)

- **Design:** Phase 3, multicentre (16 French stroke units), 1:1 randomised, double-dummy, patient-blinded and assessor-blinded, controlled, superiority trial. Treating nurses and neurologists were aware of allocation; outcome assessors (clinicians, neurologists, ophthalmologists, paramedics) and patients were masked.
- **N randomised:** 70 patients (35 alteplase, 35 aspirin) enrolled 8 June 2018 to 2 October 2023. 65 (93%) received the allocated treatment. Full analysis set 64 (91%): 34 alteplase, 30 aspirin. Primary endpoint available for 56 (88%): 29 alteplase, 27 aspirin.
- **Eligibility:** Adults ≥18 years, sudden severe persistent monocular vision loss (Snellen <20/400; >1·3 LogMAR) due to suspected non-arteritic acute CRAO confirmed by ophthalmologist (fundoscopy or retinophotography showing diffuse retinal pallor, macular cherry red spot, vessel attenuation, or visible emboli), with relative afferent pupillary defect, no GCA evidence, no stroke in past 3 months, treatable within 4·5 h of symptom onset.
- **Key exclusions:** Minor visual acuity deficits, rapid pre-treatment improvement, unknown/uncertain symptom onset time, isolated branch retinal artery occlusion without clinically significant vision loss, CRAO with foveal sparing due to cilioretinal artery (no foveal ischaemia), current anticoagulant use.
- **Intervention arm:** IV alteplase 0·9 mg/kg (10% bolus, 90% infused over 1 h, max 90 mg) + oral placebo. Same agent and dosing as ischaemic stroke.
- **Control arm:** 300 mg oral aspirin + IV saline placebo (10 mL bolus over 1 min, then 50 mL infusion over 1 h).
- **Other restrictions:** No anticoagulants, additional thrombolytics, or antiplatelets in the first 24 h after study treatment except the study product itself. Stroke unit care otherwise per European Stroke Organisation guidelines.
- **Primary outcome:** Improvement in visual acuity from baseline to 1 month of ≥0·3 LogMAR (gain of ≥3 ETDRS Snellen lines / 15 letters), analysed in the full analysis set using a generalised linear mixed model for repeated measures.
- **Secondary outcomes:** Proportion exceeding monocular blindness threshold (≤1·3 LogMAR / Snellen 20/400), functional visual recovery (≤0·5 LogMAR / Snellen 20/63), change in visual acuity over time (days 1, 7, 30, 90), visual field at 3 months, foveal threshold, mRS at 3 months, NEI-VFQ-25 quality of life at 3 months, onset-to-treatment time effect.
- **Safety:** Serious adverse events, especially symptomatic intracranial haemorrhage (ECASS II criteria) and major extracranial bleeding. Brain CT or MRI at baseline and day 1 (20–36 h) to detect ICH.
- **Statistical framework:** Frequentist binary-superiority. Sample size based on 10% improvement in aspirin vs 40% in alteplase, α 0·05, power 80%, dropout 10% → N=70. Generalised linear mixed model with logit link for repeated binary measures. Two-sided α 0·05.

## Pre-specified taxonomy

- `primaryDesign: 'binary-superiority'`
- `primaryResult: 'not-met'` — improvement ≥0·3 LogMAR at 1 month occurred in 19/29 (66%) alteplase vs 13/27 (48%) aspirin; unadjusted risk difference +17·4 pp (95% CI −11·8 to +46·5); adjusted OR 1·10 (95% CI 0·07 to 18·39); p=0·95.
- `trialResult: 'NEUTRAL'` — neutral primary efficacy with no significant superiority over aspirin; trial explicitly acknowledged underpowered. NOT a "negative" trial of alteplase per se — investigators interpret the directionally favourable numerical signal as compatible with benefit pending pooled meta-analysis.

## Primary results — verbatim from PDF p. 909 Summary

> "Among 56 patients with available data on the primary endpoint, 19 (66%) of 29 patients in the alteplase group and 13 (48%) of 27 patients in the aspirin group showed an improvement in visual acuity of at least 0·3 LogMAR at 1 month (unadjusted risk difference 17·4 [95% CI –11·8 to 46·5]; adjusted odds ratio 1·1 [95% CI 0·07 to 18·39]; p=0·95)."

> "One asymptomatic intracranial haemorrhage related to study treatment was reported in the alteplase group. 14 serious adverse events unrelated to treatment occurred in 11 patients overall (six [17%] in the aspirin group and five [14%] in the alteplase group). No symptomatic haemorrhages or major bleeding related to study treatment were reported."

## Interpretation (verbatim from PDF p. 909 Summary)

> "Intravenous alteplase administered within 4·5 h of CRAO onset was not associated with a significant improvement in visual acuity compared with aspirin, despite a higher rate of improvement in the alteplase group. However, the study was likely underpowered to detect a statistical difference. Although no safety concerns related to alteplase were identified, the overall modest recovery rates underscore the need for individual patient-level data meta-analyses with forthcoming randomised controlled trials to clarify the potential benefit of thrombolysis or aspirin in patients with acute CRAO."

## Key results table (verbatim numbers from PDF Table 2)

- **Primary, visual acuity improvement ≥0·3 LogMAR at 1 month:** alteplase 19/29 (66%) vs aspirin 13/27 (48%); adjusted OR 1·10 (0·07–18·39); p=0·95.
- **Secondary, visual acuity ≤1·3 LogMAR at 1 month (above monocular-blindness threshold):** alteplase 7/29 (24%) vs aspirin 7/27 (26%); adjusted OR 0·76 (0·01–113·33); p=0·91.
- **Secondary, visual acuity ≤0·5 LogMAR at 1 month (functional reading vision):** alteplase 4/29 (14%) vs aspirin 2/27 (7%); p=0·67 (Fisher exact).
- **mRS 0–1 at 3 months:** alteplase 17/24 (71%) vs aspirin 16/28 (57%); p=0·46.
- **NEI-VFQ-25 vision quality of life at 3 months:** alteplase 72·5 (61·2–81·4) vs aspirin 80·0 (66·5–83·7); p=0·32.
- **Visual field deficit at 3 months (dB):** alteplase −20·2 vs aspirin −25·9; p=0·35.
- **Post-hoc, visual acuity ≤0·7 LogMAR at 1 month:** alteplase 4/29 (14%) vs aspirin 4/27 (15%); p=1·00.

## Time-window subgroup (PDF Table 2, post-hoc)

- ≤3·0 h onset-to-treatment: alteplase 3/3 (100%) primary endpoint vs aspirin 1/3 (33%); n too small for inference.
- >3·0 h and ≤4·5 h: alteplase 14/24 (58%) vs aspirin 9/19 (47%).
- >4·5 h (protocol deviations): alteplase 2/2 (100%) vs aspirin 3/3 (100%).
- Post-hoc within-arm test: in the aspirin group only, earlier treatment associated with better outcome (p=0·014); in alteplase group no clear time–outcome relationship.

## Safety — verbatim from PDF Table 3 and Results

- **Symptomatic intracranial haemorrhage:** 0 in both arms.
- **Asymptomatic intracranial haemorrhage:** 1/35 (3%, alteplase) — 15 mm right parietal haematoma on day 1 CT, asymptomatic, no lesion on initial MRI. None in aspirin.
- **Fatal ICH, retinal haemorrhage, major extracranial bleeding, hypersensitivity:** 0/35 in both arms.
- **Serious adverse events after acute phase:** alteplase 5 (14%), aspirin 6 (17%) — mostly extra-ocular ischaemic events (carotid endarterectomy, pyelonephritis, melena on GI lesion, manic episode in known bipolar patient).
- **Retinal neovascularisation:** alteplase 2 (6%) vs aspirin 1 (3%) — including one intravitreal haemorrhage at 3-month follow-up.

Verbatim: "Unlike previous randomised controlled trials, no symptomatic intracranial haemorrhages were reported, and only one asymptomatic intracranial haemorrhage was identified incidentally on CT scan 24 h after onset. This prevalence of bleeding is much lower than that typically observed in patients with ischaemic stroke."

## Critical clinical context — retinal-ischaemia tolerance (PDF p. 917 Discussion)

> "The low proportion of patients with functional recovery in both treatment groups raises questions about acute retinal ischaemic tolerance, with irreversible damage potentially occurring much earlier than previously thought. Although no clear relationship between onset-to-treatment time and improvement in visual acuity was observed, only a few patients were treated within a 3·0 h window. The time threshold for permanent retinal ischaemia might have been overestimated; complete CRAO could lead to rapid and irreversible retinal ganglion cell death within as little as 15 min. These considerations highlight the need for further research to define the precise time limits for effective intervention in this patient group."

This is the central editorial caveat: even within 4·5 h the window may be too late for many CRAO patients; the durable functional recovery rate is low in both arms (≤0·5 LogMAR / Snellen 20/63: 14% alteplase, 7% aspirin).

**§8c — Guideline incorporation / subsequent statements:**
- **AHA / NANOS 2021 Scientific Statement on CRAO Management** (Mac Grory B et al., Stroke 2021; 52: e282–e294, PMID 33677974, DOI 10.1161/STR.0000000000000366). Pre-THEIA. Suggested IV tPA "may be considered" for patients with disabling visual deficits meeting criteria, based on retrospective and meta-analytic evidence (Schrag et al. 2015 IPD meta-analysis showed 50% functional recovery if treated <4·5 h). Recommended urgent triage to ED with stroke code activation, funduscopy in stroke code workflow, and GCA screening. THEIA does not yet alter this statement; THEIA's authors interpret their findings as compatible with pending IPD meta-analysis incorporating THEIA, TenCRAOS, and REVISION.
- **European Stroke Organisation (ESO) Guidelines for Management of Ischaemic Stroke and TIA 2008/updates** (Berge E et al., Eur Stroke J 2021; 6: I–LXII): provides the general AIS thrombolysis framework that THEIA inherited but does not contain CRAO-specific recommendations.
- **American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO)** Preferred Practice Pattern guidance for CRAO traditionally limited to ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis, IOP-lowering, hyperbaric oxygen; pharmacologic thrombolysis not endorsed pre-THEIA. - **Critical note:** the intersection of ophthalmology and stroke neurology is significant. THEIA is the first randomised phase 3 evidence that CRAO management belongs in the stroke pathway and that IV alteplase within 4·5 h is safe (no symptomatic ICH). Functional benefit remains unproven.

**§8d — Subsequent meta-analyses and contradicting evidence:**
- **EAGLE (Schumacher et al. 2010, Ophthalmology 2010; 117: 1367–1375, PMID 20609991, DOI 10.1016/j.ophtha.2010.03.061):** prior negative RCT of intra-arterial fibrinolysis (NOT intravenous) for CRAO. N=82 (40 conservative, 44 IA tPA). No difference in BCVA improvement at 1 month (≈0·45 LogMAR in both arms, p=0·69). IA arm had 37·1% adverse events vs 4·3%; trial terminated for safety. EAGLE is the predecessor; THEIA tests a different intervention (IV vs IA) with a much narrower window (4·5 h vs up to 20 h in EAGLE). Already registered in NeuroWiki as `eagle-trial`.
- **Schrag M, Youn T, Schindler J, Kirshner H, Greer D. Intravenous fibrinolytic therapy in central retinal artery occlusion: a patient-level meta-analysis. JAMA Neurol 2015; 72: 1148–1154** (PMID 26214143): pre-THEIA IPD meta-analysis. Cited in THEIA Methods (ref 5). Pooled cohort and case-series data suggested ~50% functional recovery with IVT <4·5 h vs ~18% untreated.
- **Mac Grory B, Nackenoff A, Poli S, et al. Intravenous fibrinolysis for central retinal artery occlusion: a cohort study and updated patient-level meta-analysis. Stroke 2020; 51: 2018–2025** (PMID 32390550, DOI 10.1161/STROKEAHA.119.028743): expanded IPD meta-analysis; 17 (50%) of 34 patients treated within 4·5 h showed visual acuity improvement to ≥0·7 LogMAR, vs 18% untreated controls.
- **Two ongoing phase 3 RCTs cited in PDF "Research in context":**
  - **TenCRAOS** (NCT04526951): tenecteplase vs placebo for CRAO. Recruiting at time of THEIA publication.
  - **REVISION** (NCT04965038): large-scale IV thrombolysis vs placebo for CRAO. Recruiting at time of THEIA publication.
- **Planned IPD meta-analysis:** THEIA authors explicitly call for an individual-patient-data meta-analysis combining THEIA + TenCRAOS + REVISION to provide level 1 evidence given THEIA's documented underpowering. This is the most likely path to a definitive answer; until then, IV alteplase for CRAO remains a Class IIb / "may be considered" intervention.

## Notable design limitations (PDF Discussion + §8 critical reading)

- **Underpowered.** Sample size N=70 calculated for 30-pp absolute improvement (40% alteplase vs 10% aspirin). Observed aspirin improvement rate was 48% (not 10%), making the trial unable to detect the smaller real difference. Authors explicitly say "the study was likely underpowered to detect a statistical difference."
- **Aspirin as comparator, not placebo.** Aspirin 300 mg was chosen over placebo to avoid delaying antiplatelet therapy in CRAO patients (≥⅓ have signs of concomitant cerebral ischaemia on DWI MRI). The aspirin improvement rate (48%) was much higher than the assumed 10%, suggesting aspirin may itself contribute to recovery in CRAO — or that the natural recovery rate was systematically higher than prior meta-analyses estimated.
- **Time-window narrow and treatment delays long.** Mean onset-to-treatment 232 min (≈3·9 h). Only 11% treated within 3·0 h. The 15-min retinal-ischaemia tolerance estimate cited by authors (Tobalem et al. 2018) suggests most patients may have been treated well after irreversible damage; this fundamentally limits any pharmacologic rescue strategy.
- **Open-label nurses/neurologists.** Double-dummy preserved patient and assessor masking, but treating staff knew allocation. Authors acknowledge potential behavioural bias (evidenced by three aspirin-group patients receiving alteplase off-protocol).
- **COVID-19 disruption + low CRAO incidence.** Recruitment extended over 5 years 4 months. Dropout 20% (vs 10% anticipated) further reduced effective power.
- **Funding declaration:** Boehringer Ingelheim co-sponsored and reviewed the manuscript for "medical and scientific accuracy related to its substances" (alteplase). Decision to publish made collaboratively by academic investigators and sponsor; confidentiality agreement specifies sponsor cannot prohibit disclosure.
