## 2. Population

**Setting:** Berlin, Germany. Berlin Fire Brigade EMS dispatch system, 15 hospital stroke units, three MSU base stations (geographic coverage rising from one MSU at start to 94% population coverage by 2018).

**Enrollment window:** February 1, 2017 to October 30, 2019 (October 30, 2019 cutoff per Design/Setting paragraph; final inclusion criteria reached May 8, 2019 — Results paragraph). 1543 patients included in adjusted primary analysis.

**Inclusion (verbatim from Methods → Patients):**
- Age ≥ 18 years
- Emergency call prompting an MSU dispatch code during MSU operating hours (7 AM–11 PM, Monday–Sunday)
- Symptom onset (or last-seen-well) to dispatch ≤ 4 hours
- Within the catchment area of a Berlin MSU
- Ambulatory before stroke (proxy for mRS ≤ 3)
- Final hospital-based diagnosis of acute cerebral ischemia (ICD-10 I63) or TIA (G45, except G45.4) without symptom resolution before arrival in either ambulance type
- Patients had to be considered eligible for thrombolysis or thrombectomy. Patients with contraindication to thrombolysis alone but considered eligible for thrombectomy were also included.

**Exclusions (verbatim from Methods):**
- Absolute contraindications to both thrombolysis and thrombectomy (i.e., no LVO patients on oral anticoagulation, no nonmedication-induced hemophilia)
- Malignancy or other severe primary disease with life expectancy < 1 year
- Major surgery within prior 4 weeks
- Previous stroke within 3 months
- Stroke mimics (final diagnosis other than ischemic stroke / TIA) — also excluded (a notable scope choice; PHANTOM-S had handled mimics separately)

**Baseline (Table 1 — Full Analysis Set, n=1543):**
- Mean age 73 years (MSU) vs 74 years (conventional); 47% women
- NIHSS at first assessment: median 4 (IQR 2–9) in both groups (low overall severity — see §8 caveat on selected population)
- LVO documented: 21.8% MSU vs 22.3% conventional
- Living at home pre-stroke: 80.2% vs 78.6%
- TIA: 16.6% vs 16.5%; ischemic stroke: 83.4% vs 83.5%

**Generalizability to NeuroWiki audience (US/global neurology residents):** The cohort is Berlin-only, single-city, public-EMS system. The median NIHSS of 4 reflects a lower-severity, well-selected population than US ED stroke cohorts. The Berlin MSU infrastructure (3 base stations across 3.8 million pop., 24/7 staffing with paramedic + radiology tech + emergency neurologist) is not directly replicable in most US or LMIC systems. Workflow takeaways are more transferable than absolute effect estimates.

## 3. Intervention and comparator (verbatim)

**Intervention exposure (Exposure paragraph + Design):**
"Simultaneous dispatch of an MSU (computed tomographic scanning with or without angiography, point-of-care laboratory testing, and thrombolysis capabilities on board) and a conventional ambulance" — n = 749 included in primary analysis.

**Comparator:**
"A conventional ambulance alone" — n = 794 included in primary analysis. Conventional ambulances staffed with paramedic + ambulance technician; transport to nearest hospital with stroke unit.

**Allocation mechanism (Methods → Study Design):** Allocation was based on **MSU availability**, not patient-level randomization. When the dispatcher entered a stroke code:
- If the emergency site was within an MSU catchment and the MSU was available → both MSU and conventional ambulance dispatched simultaneously.
- If the MSU was not available (in operation elsewhere, in maintenance, or out of catchment) → only the conventional ambulance was dispatched.
- The MSU could be cancelled en route if (a) the conventional ambulance arrived first and concluded the case was not treatable stroke, or (b) anticipated waiting time exceeded next-stroke-hospital transport time.

This is a **natural-experiment / quasi-experimental allocation**, not cluster randomization by week or shift (an earlier hypothesis in the W8.2.10 prompt). The "cluster" in B_PROUD is implicit — defined by MSU availability windows. Authors call this "a decent proxy for randomization" (Discussion); the Limitations paragraph explicitly states "the intervention in this study was not randomized; instead, patients were allocated to the MSU or conventional care groups based on availability of MSUs."

## 4. Primary endpoint (verbatim)

**Primary outcome (Methods → Outcomes):**
> "The primary outcome was the distribution of mRS ratings at 3 months."

Analyzed by ordinal logistic regression with common odds ratios, adjusted for: age, sex, hypertension, diabetes, living situation, documentation of deficits at MSU arrival, atrial fibrillation, neurological deficits at conventional-ambulance or MSU arrival, living situation before stroke. Center heterogeneity addressed by random intercepts per center. Multiple imputation (10 datasets) for missing data; proportional-odds assumption tested with Brant test; partial proportional-odds where assumption violated.

**Coprimary outcome:** 3-tier disability scale at 3 months (none-to-moderate / severe / dead) — tier assigned by mRS if available; otherwise by place of residence (home → none-to-moderate; nursing facility → severe). Introduced February 26, 2018 mid-study to handle missing mRS in roughly 9% of cases.

**Mapping to NeuroWiki current value:** `trialData.ts` shows `primaryEndpoint: { value: 'mRS Shift', label: 'at 90 Days' }`. **Match — accurate.** The current `efficacyResults` field, however, uses the coprimary "mRS 0-3 or living at home" (80.3% vs 78.0%) — that's the **coprimary**, not the primary. Display should make this distinction explicit if a binary-style chart is used.

## 5. Statistical framework

**Single primary design type:** `registry` (per skill taxonomy — "Observational; no randomization").

**Nuance for display:** This is a **prospective observational cohort with natural-experiment allocation** — closest to registry within the allowed list, but with a pre-specified hypothesis, pre-specified primary endpoint, pre-specified sample size calculation (1500 patients, 80% power to detect a Mann-Whitney probability of 0.55 from pilot mRS distributions), and pre-specified analysis plan. It is *not* a randomized superiority trial and the framework code must reflect that.

It is also not `ordinal-shift` *alone*, because that code (per skill) is reserved for ordinal-shift RCTs. B_PROUD uses an ordinal-shift analytic method on a non-randomized comparison. Recommend `statisticalFramework: 'registry'` with an explicit data-shape note: "ordinal-shift analytic method applied to non-randomized cohort" (free-text annotation, not a framework code change).

**Allowed-list candidates considered and rejected:**
- `superiority` — no, not randomized
- `ordinal-shift` — close, but reserved for RCTs in the skill taxonomy
- `workflow` — partial match (the study is in the prehospital workflow space), but the primary endpoint is patient-functional, not process
- `registry` — best fit; explicitly observational

## 6. Primary result

**Primary outcome — full analysis set (Results paragraph + Table 2):**
- Median 90-day mRS: **1 (IQR 0–3)** MSU dispatch vs **2 (IQR 0–3)** conventional ambulance — absolute median difference −1 (95% CI −3 to −0.5)
- **Adjusted common OR for worse mRS: 0.71 (95% CI 0.58–0.86), P < .001** — values < 1.00 indicate favorable shift (lower odds of higher disability)

**mRS distribution at 3 months (Figure 3A; unadjusted, %, MSU vs conventional):**
| mRS | MSU (n=654) | Conventional (n=683) | Absolute Δ (95% CI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 30.4 | 25.0 | +5.4 (0.6 to 10.2) |
| 1 | 20.5 | 17.3 | +3.2 (−1.0 to 7.4) |
| 2 | 12.1 | 14.5 | −2.4 (−6.0 to 1.2) |
| 3 | 17.4 | 19.5 | −2.1 (−6.2 to 2.1) |
| 4 | 11.6 | 13.8 | −2.2 (−5.7 to 1.4) |
| 5–6 (combined per Table 2) | 8.0 (≥5) | 10.0 (≥5) | −2.0 (−5.1 to 1.1) |

The mRS distribution per arm is fully published — both raw counts and percentages — for **n=654 MSU and n=683 conventional** (i.e., patients with non-missing mRS at 3 months). Figure 3 chart bar widths confirm.

**Coprimary outcome (Table 2, 3-month, n=730/776):**
- mRS 0–3 or living at home: 80.3% vs 78.0% (absolute Δ +2.3, 95% CI −1.8 to 6.4)
- mRS 4–5 or living in nursing institution: 12.6% vs 13.3% (Δ −0.7, 95% CI −4.1 to 2.7)
- Death: 7.1% vs 8.8% (Δ −1.7, 95% CI −4.4 to 1.1)
- **Adjusted common OR for worse coprimary outcome: 0.73 (95% CI 0.54–0.99), P = .04**

**Secondary outcomes — process and time (Table 2):**
- Patients receiving tPA: **60.2% (MSU) vs 48.1% (conventional)**; adjusted OR 1.62 (95% CI 1.32–2.00), P < .001
- Endovascular treatment: 13.8% vs 13.0% (adjusted OR 0.80, 95% CI 0.58–1.10, P = .17) — MSU did **not** significantly improve EVT rates
- Dispatch-to-imaging time, median: **45 min (MSU) vs 60 min (conventional)** — mean difference −15 min (95% CI −18 to −12), P < .001
- Onset/last-seen-well to tPA, median: 95 min vs 110 min (Δ −15 min, P = .003)
- **Dispatch-to-tPA, median: 50 min vs 70 min** — mean difference −26 min on log-transformed scale (95% CI −31 to −22), P < .001
- tPA treatment within 60 min of onset: 12.8% vs 4.0% (adjusted OR 2.96, 95% CI 1.93–4.53)

**Post-hoc time-to-treatment analysis (Results paragraph, n=687 tPA-treated):**
Compared with onset-to-treatment 181–270 min, the adjusted OR for excellent outcome (mRS 0–1) was:
- ≤ 60 min: common OR 3.25 (95% CI 1.72–6.13)
- 61–120 min: common OR 2.54 (95% CI 1.44–4.49)
- 121–180 min: common OR 2.11 (95% CI 1.12–3.94)

## 8. Expert and editorial caveats

**Accompanying editorial (same issue):** JAMA 2021;325(5):441 (referenced on the article's first page banner). Editorial title and authors not in the PDF body; if needed, retrieve separately. Confidence: MEDIUM (existence confirmed by the "Editorial page 441" callout on the article front page; content not verified from the PDF).

**Related contemporaneous trial — BEST-MSU (US):** Grotta JC, et al. NEJM 2021;385(11):971–981 (DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa2103879). Alternating-week quasi-experimental study in seven US cities. Primary outcome was utility-weighted mRS at 90 days in tPA-eligible patients; adjusted OR 2.14 for excellent outcome. Already in repo (`best-msu-trial`). **Methodological pair, not a head-to-head comparator.** Different population (tPA-eligible adjudicated subset only), different allocation (alternating-week clusters), different primary outcome (utility-weighted mRS vs full mRS distribution).

**Related trial — MR ASAP (Netherlands):** Prehospital BP-lowering RCT, not an MSU trial. Mentioned in W8.2.10 prompt as a "companion" but addresses a different prehospital intervention. **Not a direct comparator for B_PROUD design.**

**PHANTOM-S (precursor by same group):** Ebinger 2014 JAMA 311:1622–1630 — same Berlin STEMO infrastructure, earlier feasibility/process study; showed thrombolysis rate increase and time-to-treatment reduction with MSU. B_PROUD is the patient-functional-outcome follow-up.

**AHA/ASA 2026 guideline status:** Per the current `stroke-guidelines` skill (AHA/ASA 2026), MSU dispatch carries **COR IIa, LOE B-NR** for systems considering implementation, supported by B_PROUD + BEST-MSU + Kunz 2016 Lancet Neurology cohort. The recommendation is conditional on system-level cost-effectiveness analysis. **Confidence on exact 2026 wording: MEDIUM** — verify with `medical-scientist` before placing the COR/LOE in user-facing copy. The 2019 update (Powers et al., Stroke 50:e344, cited in the B_PROUD references) did not yet have a dedicated MSU class.

**Subsequent meta-analyses / systematic reviews:** The 2021 Turc et al. ESO guideline on MSUs and the Czap 2022 individual-patient-data pooled analysis (BEST-MSU + B_PROUD + Houston-MSU pilot data) confirm a consistent ordinal mRS shift favoring MSU. Confidence: MEDIUM (these are knowledge-graph references; not re-verified from primary source in this packet).

**Industry / COI disclosures:** Multiple authors (Ebinger, Siegerink, Kunz, Weber, Geisler, Freitag, Lange, Behrens, Ganeshan, Schlemm, Harmel, Lorenz-Meyer, Napierkowski, Waldschmidt, Nolte, Endres, Audebert) disclosed institutional grants from DFG (German Research Foundation) and BMBF (German Federal Ministry of Education and Research) during the study. Several disclosed personal fees from Bayer, Boehringer Ingelheim, Pfizer, Bristol Myers Squibb, Daiichi Sankyo, Stryker, Cerenovus. **No industry funding of the trial itself** — funder role explicitly stated as "no role in design and conduct."

**Limitations explicitly stated by authors (Discussion → Limitations):**
1. Not randomized — allocation by MSU availability; unmeasured confounding cannot be excluded.
2. Outcome-assessment phone interviews available for fewer than 60% of patients (the rest had written questionnaires or used residential-status proxy).
3. 9% imbalance in documentation of EMS-arrival neurological deficits between groups (higher in MSU arm).
4. Patients with final diagnoses other than acute ischemic stroke or TIA (i.e., stroke mimics, ICH) excluded — separate analyses pending.
5. Berlin-specific MSU infrastructure; generalizability to other systems uncertain.
6. Operating hours 7 AM–11 PM only — overnight strokes not covered.
7. Volunteer / motivation effect — MSU teams self-selected and highly trained; unclear how a less specialized workforce performs.
8. MSU dispatch ≠ MSU actual evaluation — 197/749 MSU dispatches were cancelled before arrival; the "MSU dispatched" group is intention-to-treat at the dispatch level, not as-treated at the patient level.

## 10. Architectural / display recommendations (for W6.6.2 + W8.2.10 decision)

### 10.1 Archetype recommendation

**Recommendation: simplified descriptive layout based on Archetype B (mRS distribution / Grotta-style stacked bars) — NOT Archetype A (dot-grid binary).**

Rationale:
- B_PROUD's primary outcome is the **full mRS distribution analyzed by ordinal shift**, not a dichotomized binary. The paper itself displays Figure 3A as a stacked bar of all seven mRS levels.
- The dichotomized coprimary (mRS 0–3 + living at home) is *available* and could power an Archetype A view, but presenting that as the primary visual misrepresents the registered primary endpoint.
- The full mRS-distribution table (§6) gives all the data shape needed for a Grotta-style bar.

**A new Archetype H (prospective-cohort) is not warranted by B_PROUD alone.** B_PROUD's data shape — ordinal mRS distribution + adjusted common OR + safety profile — is structurally identical to Archetype B (the ordinal-shift archetype used for ordinal-shift RCTs). What B_PROUD needs that a true Archetype B does not is:
1. The §1.6 design-quality disclaimer (quasi-experimental category) rendered above the chart.
2. Suppression of NNT calculation/display.
3. A `doesNotProve` block that leads with the non-randomized design.

If two or more additional prospective-cohort studies enter the corpus with the same shape (e.g., a future BEST-MSU re-analysis treated as cohort-level, or a TRIAGE-STROKE-type evaluation), the case for Archetype H strengthens. With one trial, the cost of a new archetype exceeds the benefit. **Recommend: Archetype B + §1.6 disclaimer + NNT suppression, captured as a parameter on the existing Archetype B implementation.**

This recommendation should be confirmed by `system-architect` before W8.2.10 build proceeds.

### 10.2 Proposed §1.6 design-quality disclaimer text

Category: `quasi-experimental` (per TRIALS_SPEC §1.6 enum).

**Proposed text (216 characters, within the 220-char limit):**

> "Allocation was by mobile-stroke-unit availability, not patient-level randomization. Outcome adjudication was blinded but patients and clinicians were not. Treatment effect may include unmeasured site-level confounding."

Source: paraphrased from Ebinger 2021 Limitations paragraph (page 463) and Methods → Study Design (page 455). Faithful to the published wording.

### 10.3 Proposed `doesNotProve` leader

**First sentence must lead with the design limitation, per W8.2.10 brief:**

> "Because B_PROUD allocated patients by MSU availability rather than randomization, the observed association between MSU dispatch and lower 90-day disability cannot be interpreted as a causal effect with the certainty a randomized trial would provide. Berlin-specific EMS infrastructure, daytime-only operating hours, and exclusion of stroke mimics further limit direct application to other systems."

### 10.5 NNT handling

`calculations.nnt` should either be **removed** for this record or rendered with a non-numeric placeholder + caveat. The current value (`43.5`) and explanation honestly acknowledge the limitation but the field's existence may cause UI surfaces to show "NNT 44" without context. The `clinical-trial-audit` skill is explicit: NNT is not allowed for registry/observational studies. Recommend removal; if retained, the display layer must suppress the numeric badge.
