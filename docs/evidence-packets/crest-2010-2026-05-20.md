## 2. Population

- n: 2,502 randomized and analyzed (CAS 1,262; CEA 1,240). 2,522 randomized total; one site excluded for data fabrication (20 patients removed); final ITT cohort 2,502.
- Enrollment dates: December 2000 – July 2008
- Sites: 117 (108 US, 9 Canada)
- Median follow-up: 2.5 years (planned to 4 years)
- Symptomatic / asymptomatic split: ~52.7% / ~47.3%
- Mean age: 68.9–69.2 years; ~64–66% male
- Inclusion (symptomatic): TIA, amaurosis fugax, or minor nondisabling stroke within 180 days, ipsilateral carotid stenosis ≥50% by angiography / ≥70% by ultrasound / ≥70% by CTA/MRA.
- Inclusion (asymptomatic): stenosis ≥60% by angiography / ≥70% by ultrasound / ≥80% by CTA/MRA.
- Key exclusions: previous disabling stroke; chronic atrial fibrillation.

Generalizability flags:
- All operators credentialed via a formal lead-in / certification process. Real-world CAS outside such a credentialed environment may have worse outcomes.
- North American population only; 99% white.
- "Average-risk" surgical candidates — distinct from SAPPHIRE (high-surgical-risk).

## 3. Intervention and comparator

- CAS arm: RX Acculink stent + RX Accunet distal embolic protection device (Abbott Vascular). Embolic protection required except when technically infeasible. DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel) before and after the procedure per protocol.
- CEA arm: standard carotid endarterectomy by certified surgeons; antiplatelet therapy per local practice.

## 4. Primary endpoint (verbatim)

> "The primary end point was the composite of any stroke, myocardial infarction, or death during the periprocedural period or ipsilateral stroke within 4 years after randomization."

Periprocedural window: 30 days after randomization, or 36 days if the procedure was delayed.

## 5. Statistical framework

**Primary design type:** `superiority` (mapped to enum `binary-superiority` in NeuroWiki schema; primary is a time-to-event composite but the framing per the trial-statistics skill is superiority not ordinal-shift since there is no graded outcome).

Critical correction to original task brief — CREST was NOT a noninferiority design. The paper is explicit:

> "Analyses were aimed at testing for superiority. The null hypothesis was that the two study treatments are equivalent; the alternative hypothesis was that the treatments differ."

- Sample size: 2,500
- Power: 90%
- Detection threshold: HR <0.54 or >1.49 (CAS vs CEA), ~1.2 percentage points/year absolute difference
- NI margin: none prespecified. Authors do **not** make an NI claim.
- Display archetype: `risk-table-km` (time-to-event with KM curves)
- NNT: **NOT allowed** for the primary composite (failed superiority; "no significant difference" does not generate a valid NNT)

Reconciliation note: CREST is sometimes paraphrased in textbooks as "equivalent" or "non-inferior" — that paraphrase is technically inaccurate. The trial was designed and analyzed as a superiority trial that failed to reject the null.

KM estimates of the 4-year primary composite:

| Arm | 4-year rate |
|---|---|
| CAS (stenting) | 7.2% |
| CEA (endarterectomy) | 6.8% |

## 7. Periprocedural component breakdown

Periprocedural window (30/36 day):

| Outcome | CAS | CEA | HR (95% CI) | P |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Any stroke | 4.1% | 2.3% | 1.79 (1.14–2.82) | 0.01 |
| Major ipsilateral stroke | 0.9% | 0.3% | 2.67 (0.85–8.40) | 0.09 |
| Minor ipsilateral stroke | 2.9% | 1.4% | 2.16 (1.22–3.83) | 0.009 |
| Myocardial infarction | 1.1% | 2.3% | 0.50 (0.26–0.94) | 0.03 |
| Death | 0.7% | 0.3% | 2.25 (0.69–7.30) | 0.18 |
| Cranial nerve palsy | 0.3% | 4.7% | 0.07 (0.02–0.18) | <0.001 |

Periprocedural composite (stroke + MI + death): CAS 5.2% vs CEA 4.5%, HR 1.18 (0.82–1.68), P=0.38.

4-year secondary endpoints:

| Outcome | CAS | CEA | HR | P |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Periprocedural + ipsilateral stroke at 4 years | 6.2% | 4.7% | 1.44 | 0.049 |
| Stroke or death at 4 years | 6.4% | 4.7% | 1.50 | 0.03 |
| Any stroke at 4 years | 10.2% | 7.9% | — | — |
| Death at 4 years | 11.3% | 12.6% | — | — |

**Headline tradeoff:** CAS has roughly twice the periprocedural stroke risk; CEA has roughly twice the periprocedural MI risk; cranial nerve palsy is essentially a CEA-only harm. Long-term durability of either intervention is similar.

## 10. Expert and editorial caveats — REQUIRED (filled retroactively 2026-05-20)

This section was filled retroactively after the editorial-context hard requirement landed in commit 479f100. The original packet flagged the editorial as "not retrieved this session." A subsequent evidence-verifier run filled all four sub-items.

**§10a Accompanying editorial.** Davis SM, Donnan GA. "Carotid-Artery Stenting in Stroke Prevention." NEJM 2010;363(1):80–82 (DOI 10.1056/NEJMe1004061; published with CREST on 1 July 2010). Central caveats: (1) the composite primary endpoint combined periprocedural stroke, MI, and death plus 4-year ipsilateral stroke, and this composite masked a clinically important component difference — periprocedural stroke was nearly twice as high with CAS (4.1% vs 2.3%, P=0.01) while periprocedural MI was higher with CEA (2.3% vs 1.1%, P=0.03), and the editorialists argued patients and clinicians weight a disabling stroke very differently from a troponin-defined MI, so the composite hides asymmetric harm; (2) the age-interaction finding (CEA better in patients >70, CAS comparable or better in younger patients) was hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory; (3) "more long-term data are needed before a full appreciation of the relative risks and benefits" — individualization of choice is appropriate, but equivalence at the composite level should not be read as interchangeability.

**§10b Post-publication letters and replies.** NEJM published correspondence on 21 October 2010 (NEJM 2010;363(17):1668–1671). Surviving critiques: (1) the stroke-vs-MI weighting concern (echoed from the editorial) — Brott's reply defended the composite as prespecified and clinically standard for revascularization trials, but did not contest the asymmetric impact on quality of life; (2) periprocedural MI in CREST was defined liberally (any troponin elevation with symptoms or ECG changes), generating MI events that may have lacked the prognostic weight of clinical MIs — Brott's reply pointed to subsequent CREST analyses showing periprocedural MI did carry long-term mortality risk, partially blunting but not eliminating the critique; (3) operator-experience asymmetry — CAS operators required lead-in credentialing, raising external-validity concerns for community practice. The authors acknowledged this but argued credentialing was appropriate for a novel procedure.

**§10c Subsequent guideline incorporation.** Kleindorfer DO et al., 2021 AHA/ASA Guideline for the Prevention of Stroke in Patients With Stroke and TIA (Stroke 2021;52(7):e364–e467). For *symptomatic* carotid stenosis 70–99%: CEA is recommended if perioperative stroke/death risk <6% (**Class 1, LOE A**). CAS is an alternative when CEA is contraindicated, the lesion is surgically inaccessible, or the patient has medical conditions that increase surgical risk, *and* periprocedural risk is anticipated <6% (**Class 2a, LOE B-R**) — with explicit preference for CEA in patients >70 based on the CREST age interaction. For 50–69% symptomatic stenosis, CEA is **Class 1, LOE B-R** with patient-specific factors; CAS is **Class 2b**. CREST is the primary RCT cited for the CAS recommendation level and for the age-based caveat.

**§10d Subsequent meta-analyses / contradicting evidence.** The most material follow-up is the CREST long-term analysis (Brott TG et al. NEJM 2016;374(11):1021–1031, DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1505215): at 10 years, the primary composite remained statistically indistinguishable between arms (11.8% CAS vs 9.9% CEA, HR 1.10, 95% CI 0.83–1.44), but post-procedural ipsilateral stroke beyond 30 days was identical (~6.9% in both arms), confirming that the periprocedural difference does not amplify over time. The Carotid Stenosis Trialists' Collaboration IPD meta-analysis (Bonati LH et al., Lancet 2010 and updates) pooling EVA-3S, SPACE, ICSS, and CREST reaffirmed the higher periprocedural stroke risk with CAS in patients >70 (OR ~2.0 in older patients) and supports the age-based guideline caveat. CREST-2 (asymptomatic stenosis vs intensive medical therapy, reported 2025, NEJM) is the more direct contradicting/contextualizing trial for the *asymptomatic* indication and is changing the asymptomatic-CAS recommendation landscape — it does not overturn CREST's symptomatic-stenosis conclusions.

## 11. Lineage placement

**Priors (CAS-skeptical or mixed):**
- SAPPHIRE (Yadav 2004, NEJM) — high-surgical-risk only, CAS noninferior at 30 days
- EVA-3S (Mas 2006, NEJM) — French symptomatic, halted for harm with CAS
- SPACE (Ringleb 2006, Lancet) — German/Austrian symptomatic, NI not established
- ICSS (Ederle 2010, Lancet) — international symptomatic, higher periprocedural stroke with CAS

**CREST (2010)** = first large North American trial in average-risk symptomatic and asymptomatic showing no difference on the primary 4-year composite, with the well-known signal split (CAS ↑ periprocedural stroke; CEA ↑ MI + cranial nerve palsy) and the ~70-year age-interaction.

**Subsequent / related (separate entries, do not mix data):**
- ACT-1 (Rosenfield 2016, NEJM) — asymptomatic-only, CAS noninferior at 1 year
- CREST extended follow-up (Brott TG et al., NEJM 2016;374:1021) — 10-year follow-up
- CREST-2 (NCT02089217) — CAS or CEA each vs intensive medical management in asymptomatic ≥70% stenosis; results awaited as of 2026-05-20
