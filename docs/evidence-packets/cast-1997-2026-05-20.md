- **Journal:** Lancet

- **Funding:** Multiple — Chinese collaborators + UK Medical Research Council CTSU support

## Trial identity (verified from PDF)

- **Design:** Large, randomised, **double-blind, placebo-controlled** trial (envelopes prepacked centrally with calendar-packed aspirin or matching placebo; 1:1 allocation, balanced for every 10 consecutive patients within each hospital)
- **N randomised:** 21,106 patients (413 Chinese hospitals, November 1993–March 1997; 10,554 aspirin vs 10,552 placebo)
- **Eligibility:** Suspected acute ischaemic stroke within 48 h of onset; no contraindication to aspirin (recent gastric bleeding, allergy); reasonable certainty haemorrhagic stroke could be excluded. CT before randomisation mandatory only for comatose patients; 87% had a CT scan before randomisation; 94% had at least one in-hospital CT. Uncertainty principle.
- **Intervention:** Aspirin 160 mg/day (single film-coated tablet, crushed or chewed for first dose; given via nasogastric tube if patient could not swallow) vs matching placebo, for up to 4 weeks (or until earlier discharge or death)
- **Outcomes:**
  - Primary: (a) death from any cause during the scheduled treatment period (in-hospital, up to 4 weeks); (b) death or dependence at discharge
  - Pre-specified secondary: fatal or non-fatal recurrent stroke; death or non-fatal stroke during treatment period; transfused or fatal extracranial haemorrhage; pulmonary embolism
- **Statistical framework:** Frequentist binary-superiority. Pre-specified to be analysed in parallel with the concurrent IST. ITT analysis.
- **Trial completeness:** Discharge forms available for 20,655/21,106 (97.9%); 97% in each arm completed 4 weeks of trial tablets or took them throughout hospital stay until death/discharge.
- **Final diagnosis:** Ischaemic stroke confirmed in ~98% of randomised patients; 174 (0.8%) had haemorrhagic strokes at final diagnosis; 128 (0.6%) had brain tumours or other diseases — all included in ITT analysis.

## Pre-specified taxonomy

- `primaryDesign: 'binary-superiority'`
- `primaryResult: 'met'` for the mortality primary (3.3% aspirin vs 3.9% placebo; 2p=0.04) AND for the combined in-hospital death or non-fatal stroke endpoint (5.3% vs 5.9%; 2p=0.03). The "dead or dependent at discharge" co-primary was directionally favourable (30.5% vs 31.6%, 11.4 fewer per 1000) but did not reach 2p<0.05 (2p=0.08).
- `trialResult: 'POSITIVE'` — primary mortality endpoint met; combined endpoint met; trial considered positive in the canonical sense and is cited alongside IST as the foundational aspirin-in-AIS evidence.

## Primary results (verbatim from PDF, p. 1641 Summary)

> "There was a significant 14% (SD 7) proportional reduction in mortality during the scheduled treatment period (343 [3.3%] deaths among aspirin-allocated patients vs 398 [3.9%] deaths among placebo-allocated patients; 2p=0.04). There were significantly fewer recurrent ischaemic strokes in the aspirin-allocated than in the placebo-allocated group (167 [1.6%] vs 215 [2.1%]; 2p=0.01) but slightly more haemorrhagic strokes (115 [1.1%] vs 93 [0.9%]; 2p>0.1). For the combined in-hospital endpoint of death or non-fatal stroke at 4 weeks, there was a 12% (6) proportional risk reduction with aspirin (545 [5.3%] vs 614 [5.9%]; 2p=0.03), an absolute difference of 6.8 (3.2) fewer cases per 1000."

> "At discharge, 3153 (30.5%) aspirin-allocated patients and 3266 (31.6%) placebo-allocated patients were dead or dependent, corresponding to 11.4 (6.4) fewer per 1000 in favour of aspirin (2p=0.08)."

## Key absolute differences per 1000 (verbatim from PDF Results, pp. 1644–1646)

- **In-hospital mortality (4 weeks):** −5.4 (SD 2.6) per 1000 (3.3% vs 3.9%, 2p=0.04)
- **Recurrent ischaemic stroke (in-hospital):** −4.7 (1.9) per 1000 (1.6% vs 2.1%, 2p=0.01)
- **Haemorrhagic stroke (in-hospital):** +2.1 (1.4) per 1000 (1.1% vs 0.9%, 2p>0.1, NS)
- **Combined death or non-fatal stroke (in-hospital):** −6.8 (3.2) per 1000 (5.3% vs 5.9%, 2p=0.03)
- **Dead or dependent at discharge:** −11.4 (6.4) per 1000 (30.5% vs 31.6%, 2p=0.08; trend, did not reach conventional significance)
- **Transfused or fatal extracranial bleeds:** +2.7 (1.2) per 1000 (0.8% vs 0.6%, 2p=0.02; restricted-to-survivors analysis: +1.9 [0.8] per 1000)

## Interpretation (verbatim from PDF, p. 1641 Summary + Discussion)

> "There are two major trials of aspirin in acute ischaemic stroke. Taken together, CAST and the similarly large IST show reliably that aspirin started early in hospital produces a small but definite net benefit, with about 9 (SD 3) fewer deaths or non-fatal strokes per 1000 in the first few weeks (2p=0.001), and with 13 (5) fewer dead or dependent per 1000 after some weeks or months of follow-up (2p<0.01)."

> "Hence, aspirin treatment should now be considered for almost all patients presenting with acute ischaemic stroke, provided that there are no strong contraindications and that haemorrhagic stroke can be excluded with reasonable probability, whether based on CT scan or not."

## Joint IST + CAST pooled signal (verbatim from CAST Discussion, p. 1647)

> "In these trials, which included a wide range of patients, aspirin caused about two (SD 1) haemorrhagic strokes among every 1000 patients treated, but prevented about 11 (SD 3) other strokes or deaths in hospital. Almost all the difference in fatal or non-fatal recurrent ischaemic or unknown stroke was confined to ischaemic stroke (322 aspirin vs 457 control cases), a difference of 6.7 (SD 1.4) per 1000."

## Limitations (paraphrased from PDF Discussion, pp. 1646–1648)

- **Chinese-only enrolment:** all 413 hospitals were in mainland China. Stroke subtype distribution: more intracranial atherosclerosis and small-vessel/lacunar; less extracranial carotid disease; less cardioembolic stroke than in Western populations. Authors note (p. 1647): "in CAST 72% were younger than 70, whereas in IST only 38% were under age 70" — case-mix and age distribution differ substantially from IST.
- **In-hospital outcome only:** primary endpoint was during the scheduled 4-week treatment period or until discharge; longer-term follow-up not protocolised (vs IST's 6-month follow-up). 6-month or 90-day mRS not available from CAST alone.
- **Co-treatments:** 53% of CAST patients received a calcium antagonist; 51% received Chinese herbal products. No substantial benefit for these treatments is established but background-therapy heterogeneity is a generalizability caveat.
- **Modified Rankin Scale recorded at discharge:** disability assessed at variable in-hospital time-point (typical hospital stay ~4 weeks), not at a fixed post-stroke timepoint as in IST 6-month follow-up.
- **No written consent required** (protocol exception for emergency setting; verbal discussion with patient/relatives at clinician discretion) — modern ethics frameworks would not permit this design, but it was protocol-specified and approved at the time.
- **Pre-thrombolytic era:** CAST enrolled 1993–1997. Alteplase was not routine in China in this era. Modern: aspirin delayed 24 h after thrombolysis.
- **Aspirin dose 160 mg/day:** lower than the IST 300 mg/day and lower than common US 325 mg loading. The pooled IST+CAST analysis covers the 160-325 mg range.
- **1.5% misdiagnoses at randomisation:** 174 haemorrhagic strokes and 128 brain tumours/other diseases were diagnosed in the final analysis. ITT analysis confirmed no signal of net harm in this misdiagnosed subgroup — supports the practical recommendation that aspirin can be initiated when CT is not immediately available, provided ICH is reasonably excluded.

## Geographic generalizability

- Mainland Chinese hospital population. Stroke subtypes more weighted toward intracranial atherosclerosis and lacunar stroke. Lower in-hospital mortality than IST (3.6% pooled vs ~9% in IST) — partly age (72% under 70 in CAST vs 38% under 70 in IST), partly subtype, partly exclusion of severe stroke (Chinese clinicians more reluctant to enrol moribund or NG-tube-requiring patients).
- The joint IST + CAST signal (40,000 patients across both East Asian and Western populations) is what underwrites global guideline applicability.

## Conclusion (per medical-scientist)

CAST established that early aspirin (160 mg/day, within 48 h of acute ischaemic stroke) produces a small but statistically significant reduction in in-hospital mortality (3.3% vs 3.9%, ARR 5.4 per 1000), in recurrent ischaemic stroke (1.6% vs 2.1%), and in the combined endpoint of death or non-fatal stroke at 4 weeks (5.3% vs 5.9%). The increase in haemorrhagic stroke (+2 per 1000) is small and non-significant in CAST alone. Taken with the parallel IST (n=19,435), CAST contributes to a ~40,000-patient evidence base supporting the AHA/ASA Class I, Level A recommendation for aspirin within 24-48 h of acute ischaemic stroke once ICH is excluded. The pooled analysis (Chen et al. 2000) is the canonical reference modern guidelines cite.
