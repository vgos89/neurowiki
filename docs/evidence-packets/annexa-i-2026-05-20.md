## §2 Trial design (verified)

- **Type:** Multicenter, multinational, open-label, randomized controlled trial with blinded outcome adjudication. Treatment was unblinded at sites; steering-committee members and end-point adjudicators were blinded.
- **Sites:** 131 sites across 23 countries.
- **Enrollment period:** June 6, 2019 – May 27, 2023.
- **N:** 530 randomized (extended population). Primary efficacy analysis (interim, pre-specified): 452 patients (224 andexanet, 228 usual care). Safety analysis: all 530.
- **Randomization:** 1:1, stratified by intention to use prothrombin complex concentrate and (after amendment 1) by time from symptom onset to baseline imaging (<180 or ≥180 minutes).
- **Sample size and early stop:** Originally 900 patients planned. Amendment introduced an interim analysis at 450 patients with a pre-specified significance threshold of P<0.031. On May 31, 2023, the DSMB recommended that the trial be stopped after the interim analysis at 452 patients met the criterion for efficacy. **Trial was halted early for hemostatic efficacy.**

## §3 Population

**Inclusion:**
- Acute intracerebral hemorrhage with most recent FXa-inhibitor dose within 15 hours before randomization.
- Hematoma volume 0.5–60 mL on CT or MRI within 2 hours before randomization.
- NIHSS ≤35.
- After amendment: only primary intracerebral (not subdural or subarachnoid) hemorrhage was eligible; amendment also tightened the symptom-onset window from ≤12 h to ≤6 h to baseline scan.

**Exclusion:**
- GCS <7 at consent.
- NIHSS >35.
- Planned surgery within 12 h after enrollment.
- Thrombotic event within prior 2 weeks.
- Prior andexanet exposure.

**Baseline:** Mean age ~78.9 years. Atrial fibrillation in ~87% (90.2% andexanet vs 84.2% usual care). Most frequent FXa inhibitor: apixaban (~60%), then rivaroxaban (~29%), edoxaban (~10%). Median hematoma volume 10.5 mL (andexanet) vs 9.0 mL (usual care). Median NIHSS 9 in both arms. Median time from symptom onset to baseline scan ~2.3–2.4 h.

## §4 Intervention

- **Andexanet arm:** High-dose bolus or low-dose bolus (per FDA label, determined by FXa-inhibitor type, amount, and timing of last dose) given over 15–30 min, followed by continuous infusion over 2 h. Vial contains 500 mg of mannitol per vial. In the primary efficacy population, 78.1% received low-dose; 20.1% received high-dose.
- **Usual-care arm:** Per local physician judgment; andexanet excluded. PCC permitted. 85.5% of usual-care patients received PCC within the first 3 h (median dose 3000 IU, IQR 2000–3500). Of these, 92.4% received four-factor PCC.

## §5 Endpoints (verified)

**Primary outcome — hemostatic efficacy (composite):** All three must be met at 12 h after randomization:
1. Change in hematoma volume ≤35% from baseline (≤20% = "excellent"; ≤35% = "good"), AND
2. Increase in NIHSS score <7 points at 12 h, AND
3. No receipt of rescue therapy (andexanet, PCC, or surgery) between 3 and 12 h after randomization.

**Secondary outcome:** Percent change from baseline to 1-to-2-h nadir in anti-FXa activity.

**Safety outcomes:** Thrombotic events and death within 30 days. Assessed in extended population (530 patients).

**Exploratory:** mRS at 30 days (post hoc, dichotomized 0–3 vs 4–6).

## §6 Statistical analysis (verified)

- Primary analysis: Cochran-Mantel-Haenszel test stratified by symptom-onset-to-baseline-scan time (<180 or ≥180 min). Two-sided α=0.031 at the interim analysis.
- Hierarchical testing procedure for primary and secondary end points to preserve family-wise α=5%.
- Efficacy analyses: intention-to-treat in primary efficacy population (n=452).
- Safety analyses: all patients who received andexanet or usual care; included a few subdural/subarachnoid patients enrolled before amendment 1 (n=530 extended population).
- mRS at 30 days reported as exploratory/post-hoc — trial not powered for functional outcome; multiplicity unadjusted.

**Statistical design taxonomy (per `.claude/skills/trial-statistics`):** Binary superiority trial (composite primary endpoint, dichotomous). Primary result met — stopped early for efficacy at pre-specified interim. NNT computable from the primary ARD (13.4 pp). Trial was **stopped early for benefit** — required caveat: "Stopped early for benefit; effect size may be overestimated."

## §8 Editorial / post-publication / guideline context

### §8a Accompanying NEJM editorial (verified)
- Smith WS, Hemphill JC. "Reversing Oral Anticoagulation in Intracerebral Hemorrhage." N Engl J Med 2024;390:1815–1816. DOI: 10.1056/NEJMe2403726.
- Key points raised: timing and severity of ischemic strokes in the trial were not described in detail; relevance of atrial-fibrillation–related transient hypercoagulability after andexanet; primary outcome was hemostatic surrogate, not functional; functional and mortality outcomes did not differ at 30 days; longer-term outcomes not reported.

### §8b Post-publication letters and commentary
- Multiple critical commentaries (REBEL EM, First10EM, SGEM) raise concerns about: cost-effectiveness (~US$23,000/dose); thrombotic-stroke trade-off; lack of functional benefit at 30 days; surrogate primary outcome.
- Note: ANNEXA-I has been widely discussed but a formal NEJM correspondence cluster equivalent to that of NINDS or ECASS III has not been catalogued in the V-supplied source set; document this as a follow-up to verify before final ship.

### §8c Subsequent guideline incorporation
- The **AHA/ASA 2022 ICH Guideline** (Greenberg et al., Stroke 2022) pre-dates ANNEXA-I but had already assigned andexanet a Class IIa, Level B recommendation for FXa-inhibitor reversal in acute ICH based on pre-ANNEXA-I evidence (ANNEXA-4 single-arm and observational data).
- ESO 2022 guideline similarly recommends andexanet or 4F-PCC for FXa-inhibitor-associated ICH (weak recommendation, low-quality evidence).
- Post-ANNEXA-I focused updates from AHA/ASA and ESO are pending as of 2026-05-20 per V-supplied sources; a focused update incorporating ANNEXA-I is anticipated but **not yet verified in this packet.** Document as TODO-VERIFY for the next freshness review.

## §9 Verbatim quoted text for citation record

From the abstract (verbatim):

> "Among patients with intracerebral hemorrhage who were receiving factor Xa inhibitors, andexanet resulted in better control of hematoma expansion than usual care but was associated with thrombotic events, including ischemic stroke."

(Connolly SJ et al. NEJM 2024;390:1745–1755.)

## §10 Critical limitations and caveats

1. **Stopped early for benefit (DSMB-halted interim analysis).** Effect size may be overestimated. The trial did not reach planned full enrollment of 900.
2. **Open-label design.** Treatment assignment was unblinded at sites; only end-point adjudicators were blinded.
3. **Composite primary endpoint** combining hematoma volume change, NIHSS change, and rescue-therapy avoidance. Between-group difference was driven primarily by hematoma-volume control; the NIHSS-change and rescue-therapy components did not differ significantly between groups.
4. **Surrogate primary endpoint.** Hemostatic efficacy is a radiographic/biochemical composite, not a functional or mortality outcome.
5. **No functional benefit at 30 days.** Exploratory mRS 0–3 at 30 days: 28.0% andexanet vs 31.0% usual care (numerically favoring usual care, not significant).
6. **No mortality benefit.** 30-day mortality 27.8% vs 25.5% (P=0.51).
7. **Increased thrombotic events,** primarily driven by ischemic stroke (6.5% vs 1.5%). This is the central clinical trade-off.
8. **Comparator was "usual care," not protocolized PCC.** 85.5% of usual-care patients received PCC, but timing, dose, and product varied.
9. **Cost.** Andexanet is substantially more expensive than 4F-PCC; cost-effectiveness is a documented concern in editorial and observational follow-up literature.
10. **Population restrictions:** ≤15 h from FXa-inhibitor dose, ≤60 mL hematoma, no planned surgery, NIHSS ≤35. Do not generalize beyond these bounds.
11. **Generalizability to non-ICH FXa-inhibitor bleeding:** This trial addresses **only intracerebral hemorrhage**. ANNEXA-4 (single-arm, 2019) addresses other major bleeding; that evidence is separate and does not transfer.
