## Section 4.9 — Synopsis (verbatim, pages e68–e69)

> "Anticoagulation (the pharmacological inhibition of the coagulation cascade with IV or oral medications) has been tested extensively in the treatment of AIS. The underlying principle is that anticoagulation may prevent early recurrent stroke, prevent neurological worsening, reduce the risk of thrombus propagation, and potentially counter the phenomenon of incomplete microcirculatory reperfusion. However, in general, these theoretical benefits have not been borne out in the medical literature. Among patients with AIS and AF who are selected for anticoagulation poststroke, a strategy of early initiation of a DOAC rather than delayed initiation is safe, although the efficacy of this approach in early recurrent stroke prevention is not established."

## Section 4.9 — Recommendation-Specific Supportive Text, Item 1 (verbatim, page e69)

This is the single most important block for the ELAN pathway. Reproduced verbatim:

> "**1.** In the Early versus Late Initiation of Direct Oral Anticoagulants in Post-Ischemic Stroke Patients with Atrial Fibrillation (ELAN) trial, 'early anticoagulation' (within 48 hours of AIS onset in minor/moderate AIS and on day 6/7 in those with major AIS) versus 'later anticoagulation' (day 3/4 after a minor AIS, day 6/7 after moderate AIS, or day 12–14 after major AIS) led to a numerical (but not statistically significant) reduction in recurrent AIS, systemic embolism, major bleeding, sICH, or vascular death (risk difference, −1.18 [95% CI, −2.84 to 0.47]). In the Optimal Timing of Anticoagulation After AIS with Atrial Fibrillation (OPTIMAS) trial, 3648 participants with AIS and AF were randomized to 'early' DOAC therapy (≤4 days of onset) or 'delayed' DOAC therapy (7–14 days of onset). Early DOAC therapy was noninferior to delayed DOAC therapy. The Timing of Oral Anticoagulant Therapy in AIS with Atrial Fibrillation (TIMING) trial randomized participants to early (≤4 days of onset) or delayed (5–10 days from onset) anticoagulation. The primary outcome occurred in 6.9% of participants with early anticoagulation and 8.7% of participants with delayed anticoagulation, suggesting that early anticoagulation was noninferior to delayed anticoagulation."

This is the only place in the guideline where ELAN's day-windows (≤48h for minor/moderate, day 6/7 for major, day 12–14 comparator for major) appear verbatim.

## Section 4.9 — Knowledge Gaps and Future Research (verbatim excerpts relevant to ELAN, page e70)

> "There is a lack of prediction tools to aid in selecting patients for early as opposed to delayed anticoagulation in patients with AIS and AF. The ELAN trial indicated that early anticoagulation may be superior to delayed anticoagulation. In this trial, participants were subdivided based on whether their index stroke was 'mild,' 'moderate,' or 'severe' based on infarct sizes and locations. However, it is likely that there may be other patient-specific factors that could be used to identify those at especially high risk of early recurrence (which may indicate that early anticoagulation is preferable) or at especially high risk of hemorrhagic complications (which may indicate that delayed anticoagulation is preferable)."

> "There may be special populations in whom early anticoagulation (within 48 hours) is warranted. Although a strategy of early anticoagulation (within 48 hours) in patients with undifferentiated stroke is not recommended, there may be a population of patients in whom such a strategy is warranted. Such patients may include those with intracardiac thrombi (within the left atrium or left ventricle), patients with left ventricular assist devices, or those with mechanical cardiac valves (aortic or mitral)."

> "There is a lack of high-level evidence on concomitant full-dose anticoagulation (LMWH or DOACs) with IVT."

## Section 4.9 — How the guideline does NOT speak about ELAN

This is the critical finding for the pathway audit:

- **AHA/ASA 2026 does NOT publish a day-by-day timing table** stratified by infarct size (TIA/minor/moderate/major) with specific "Day 0–2 vs Day 3–4 vs Day 6–7 vs Day 12–14" recommendations.
- **The day-windows displayed in NeuroWiki's ELAN pathway** (≤48h for TIA/minor/moderate, day 6/7 for major, day 12–14 comparator) **are ELAN trial protocol values reproduced verbatim by the guideline narrative**, NOT a guideline-issued COR/LOE recommendation.
- **The guideline recommendation (Rec #1, COR 2a, LOE A)** is broader and softer: "*early oral anticoagulation poststroke … is reasonable compared with … delayed anticoagulation*" in "carefully selected (eg, milder severity) patients."
- **The "carefully selected" / "milder severity" qualifier matters.** The COR 2a/LOE A label applies primarily to milder strokes. For major strokes, the guideline does not separately endorse the day 6/7 ELAN window with its own COR — it cites it as the protocol value used in ELAN.

This is a **valid finding, not a failure**: AHA 2026 DOES have ELAN guidance (Rec 1, COR 2a, LOE A, with all three trials — ELAN, OPTIMAS, TIMING — discussed by name in the supportive text). The pathway's claim that it operationalizes "the ELAN framework within AHA/ASA 2026 Class 2a recommendation" is accurate. What is NOT in the guideline is a day-by-day timing table at COR-level granularity — that lives in the ELAN trial protocol itself.

## Findings — codebase vs PDF

### Finding 1 — TIA inclusion extends beyond ELAN trial population (severity: low–medium)

**Location:** `src/pages/ElanPathway.tsx` lines 79–84, 231 ("Ischemic Stroke or TIA with Atrial Fibrillation?"), 192 ("transient ischemic attack or no persistent infarct").

**Issue:** ELAN enrolled patients with **acute ischemic stroke** (AIS) and AF. TIA was not a trial inclusion. The AHA 2026 guideline (§4.9 Rec 1) speaks to "AIS with atrial fibrillation." The pathway permits TIA as an eligible category and assigns it the same timing windows as a minor stroke (≤48h early / day 3–4 late).

**Defensibility:** TIA + AF in clinical practice usually triggers immediate or near-immediate anticoagulation regardless of pathway. The day-3–4 "later" window for TIA is a reasonable clinical extrapolation but is **not in ELAN's trial protocol and not in AHA 2026**.

**Suggested correction:** Either (a) tighten TIA copy to say "TIA with AF (extrapolation beyond ELAN inclusion)" with a disclaimer, or (b) remove the TIA category and reroute TIA users to a separate disclaimer-only screen indicating that TIA falls outside the ELAN protocol and that early DOAC initiation is typically appropriate. Option (a) is lower-risk.

### Finding 2 — Imaging-based size definitions sourced from ELAN trial protocol, not AHA 2026 (severity: low — attribution only)

**Location:** `src/pages/ElanPathway.tsx` lines 192–195 (in `copySummary`), lines 293–308 (size definition cards).

**Issue:** The size taxonomy — Minor ≤1.5 cm, Moderate = cortical superficial branch / deep MCA / internal border-zone, Major = large territory or brainstem/cerebellum >1.5 cm — is reproduced verbatim from the ELAN trial protocol (NEJM 2023, Supplementary Appendix). The AHA 2026 guideline narrative does not publish these thresholds; it only names "mild / moderate / severe based on infarct sizes and locations" (§4.9 Knowledge Gaps, e70).

**Defensibility:** Using the trial's own size taxonomy is the correct operational choice when reproducing the trial's timing windows. The problem is attribution — the pathway's UI implies these definitions are guideline-sourced. They are trial-sourced.

**Suggested correction:** Add a small attribution line to the size-definition card section: "Size definitions per ELAN trial protocol (Fischer et al., NEJM 2023)." This is a non-clinical, attribution-only change.

### Finding 3 — Header label "COR 2a · LOE A" applied universally to all size strata, but guideline narrative qualifies "carefully selected (eg, milder severity)" (severity: medium)

**Location:** `src/pages/ElanPathway.tsx` lines 374–375, header rendered on every result regardless of size.

**Issue:** The COR 2a / LOE A label in §4.9 Rec 1 is qualified verbatim by **"In carefully selected (eg, milder severity) patients."** The pathway displays "COR 2a · LOE A" identically for TIA, Minor, Moderate, AND Major strokes. For Major strokes (day 6/7 early window, day 12–14 late), the guideline does not separately assign COR 2a — the recommendation language emphasizes "milder severity." The day 6/7 / day 12–14 windows for major stroke are ELAN protocol values, not guideline-affirmed COR 2a windows.

**Defensibility:** Strict reading. A more permissive reading is that the COR 2a applies to "early initiation of DOAC after AIS+AF" generally, with milder severity as the named example, not the strict subset. The supportive text item 1 discusses all severity strata (minor/moderate/major) under the umbrella of Rec 1.

**Suggested correction:** For the **Major** stroke result card, change the header subtitle to read something like "ELAN protocol framework; AHA 2026 COR 2a applies primarily to selected milder-severity patients." This preserves the COR 2a label but adds the qualifier. This is the highest-priority correction in this audit.

### Finding 4 — ELAN primary outcome not statistically significant; pathway evidence panel could be more transparent (severity: low)

**Location:** `src/pages/ElanPathway.tsx` lines 443 ("ELAN Trial" detail string).

**Issue:** The "Learn more" accordion describes ELAN as: *"Early DOAC used within 48 hours for TIA/minor/moderate events and day 6-7 for major stroke, versus a later comparator strategy. This tool operationalizes those ELAN timing bins."* This is accurate but does not state that ELAN's composite primary outcome did NOT reach statistical significance (RD −1.18, 95% CI −2.84 to 0.47, per AHA 2026 §4.9 supportive text item 1).

**Defensibility:** OPTIMAS and TIMING both established noninferiority cleanly. The clinical message — early DOAC is safe and reasonable — survives intact. But the ELAN-specific statistical caveat ("numerical but not statistically significant") is a fair-paraphrase requirement.

**Suggested correction:** Append to the ELAN trial detail: "Primary composite outcome was numerically lower with early initiation but did not reach statistical significance (RD −1.18, 95% CI −2.84 to 0.47); OPTIMAS and TIMING subsequently confirmed noninferiority."

### Finding 5 — "Noninferior to delayed start (COR 2a)" caption on the Earlier Strategy column (severity: low)

**Location:** `src/pages/ElanPathway.tsx` line 386.

**Issue:** The caption "Noninferior to delayed start (COR 2a)" is accurate for OPTIMAS and TIMING (both established NI). It is **not** accurate as a description of the ELAN trial itself (which did not formally establish statistical NI on its composite primary outcome). The conflation is mild because the AHA 2026 guideline language synthesizes all three trials under the same recommendation.

**Defensibility:** The COR 2a recommendation is a synthesis-level statement and incorporates OPTIMAS + TIMING + ELAN. Calling the strategy "noninferior" at the COR 2a level is fair. Calling it noninferior with ELAN as the only attribution would be a misstatement.

**Suggested correction:** Either accept current copy (since COR 2a synthesizes all three trials) or clarify to "Noninferior to delayed start in OPTIMAS / TIMING (COR 2a)." Lower priority than Finding 3.
