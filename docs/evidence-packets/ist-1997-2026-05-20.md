## Bibliographic identity (verified from PDF + PubMed)

- **Title (full):** "The International Stroke Trial (IST): a randomised trial of aspirin, subcutaneous heparin, both, or neither among 19 435 patients with acute ischaemic stroke"
- **Author/group:** International Stroke Trial Collaborative Group (lead Sandercock PAG, Oxford CTSU)
- **Journal:** Lancet
- **Date / volume / pages:** 1997 May 31; 349 (9065): 1569–1581
- **DOI:** 10.1016/S0140-6736(97)04011-7
- **PMID:** 9174558
- **Funding:** Multiple — declared in source paper; UK MRC, Stroke Association, others

## Trial identity (verified from PDF)

- **Design:** Large, randomised, **open** (unblinded), **factorial 2×2** trial. Half allocated subcutaneous unfractionated heparin (5000 IU bd low-dose OR 12500 IU bd medium-dose) vs "avoid heparin"; in factorial, half allocated aspirin 300 mg/day vs "avoid aspirin". No placebo arms.
- **N randomised:** 19,435 patients (467 hospitals, 36 countries; pilot 1991–1993, main trial 1993–1996)
- **Eligibility:** Suspected acute ischaemic stroke within 48 hours of onset; no evidence of ICH; no clear contraindication. Uncertainty principle. 67% had CT before randomisation; 29% scanned after; 4% never scanned.
- **Treatment duration:** Up to 14 days (or until earlier discharge)
- **Outcomes:**
  - Primary co-outcomes: (a) death from any cause within 14 days; (b) death or dependency at 6 months
  - Pre-specified secondary: symptomatic ICH ≤14 days, recurrent ischaemic stroke ≤14 days, major extracranial haemorrhage, pulmonary embolism, death by 6 months, dependency or incomplete recovery at 6 months
- **Statistical framework:** Frequentist binary-superiority. Two main analyses: "immediate heparin (any dose) vs avoid heparin" AND "immediate aspirin vs avoid aspirin". Prospectively planned to be analysed together with CAST.
- **Follow-up completeness:** 14-day outcome 99.99% complete; 6-month outcome 99.2% complete

## Pre-specified taxonomy

- `primaryDesign: 'binary-superiority'`
- `primaryResult: 'not-met'` for the unadjusted 6-month death-or-dependency endpoint with aspirin (2p=0.07 unadjusted; 2p=0.03 adjusted for baseline prognosis — borderline, did not meet pre-specified primary threshold without adjustment). However, the 14-day combined endpoint (death or recurrent non-fatal stroke) did meet conventional significance (11.3% vs 12.4%, 2p=0.02).
- `trialResult: 'POSITIVE'` (aspirin arm) — primary verdict per modern guideline citation: the IST aspirin arm is the foundational evidence (alongside CAST) for early aspirin within 48 h of acute ischaemic stroke; the combined trial signal is unambiguous.

## Primary results — aspirin vs avoid aspirin (verbatim from PDF, p. 1569 Summary + Results)

> "Aspirin-allocated patients had significantly fewer recurrent ischaemic strokes within 14 days (2.8% vs 3.9%) with no significant excess of haemorrhagic strokes (0.9% vs 0.8%), so the reduction in death or non-fatal recurrent stroke with aspirin (11.3% vs 12.4%) was significant."

> "At 6 months there was a non-significant trend towards a smaller percentage of the aspirin group being dead or dependent (62.2% vs 63.5%, 2p=0.07), a difference of 13 (SD 7) per 1000; after adjustment for baseline prognosis the benefit from aspirin was significant (14 [SD 6] per 1000, 2p=0.03)."

> "Aspirin was associated with a significant excess of 5 (SD 1) transfused or fatal extracranial bleeds per 1000; in the absence of heparin the excess was 2 (SD 1) and was not significant."

## Primary results — heparin vs avoid heparin (verbatim from PDF, p. 1569 Summary)

> "Patients allocated to heparin had significantly fewer recurrent ischaemic strokes within 14 days (2.9% vs 3.8%) but this was offset by a similar-sized increase in haemorrhagic strokes (1.2% vs 0.4%), so the difference in death or non-fatal recurrent stroke (11.7% vs 12.0%) was not significant. Heparin was associated with a significant excess of 9 (SD 1) transfused or fatal extracranial bleeds per 1000."

> "At 6 months the percentage dead or dependent was identical in both groups (62.9%)."

## Interpretation (verbatim from PDF, p. 1569)

> "Neither heparin regimen offered any clinical advantage at 6 months. The results suggest that if heparin is given in routine clinical practice, the dose should not exceed 5000 IU subcutaneously twice daily. For aspirin, the IST suggests a small but worthwhile improvement at 6 months. Taking the IST together with the comparably large Chinese Acute Stroke Trial, aspirin produces a small but real reduction of about 10 deaths or recurrent strokes per 1000 during the first few weeks."

## Key absolute differences per 1000 (verbatim from PDF p. 1569 + Results)

- **Aspirin vs no aspirin, 14-day death or recurrent non-fatal stroke:** 11 fewer per 1000 (2p=0.02)
- **Aspirin vs no aspirin, 6-month death or dependence (unadjusted):** 13 (SD 7) fewer per 1000, 2p=0.07
- **Aspirin vs no aspirin, 6-month death or dependence (adjusted for baseline prognosis):** 14 (SD 6) fewer per 1000, 2p=0.03
- **Aspirin vs no aspirin, transfused or fatal extracranial bleeds:** +5 (SD 1) per 1000 (with heparin); +2 (SD 1) per 1000 (without heparin, NS)
- **Heparin vs no heparin, 14-day haemorrhagic stroke:** +8 per 1000 (1.2% vs 0.4%, 2p<0.00001)
- **Heparin vs no heparin, transfused or fatal extracranial bleeds:** +9 (SD 1) per 1000 (2p<0.00001)
- **Medium-dose vs low-dose heparin (subsidiary):** medium-dose 14-day death or non-fatal stroke 12.6% vs low-dose 10.8%, 2p=0.007 (medium-dose worse)

## Limitations (verbatim and paraphrased from PDF Discussion, pp. 1577–1579)

- **Open-label, no placebo control** — the IST was unblinded; bias in unblinded assessment of secondary outcomes possible. Authors blinded the 6-month assessor in most countries; pilot data confirmed patients could not recall allocation at 6 months.
- **Aspirin dose 300 mg — not modern 81 mg or 325 mg** — IST used 300 mg/day. CAST used 160 mg/day. Modern North American dosing is 325 mg loading then 81 mg maintenance; modern European dosing is 75–100 mg. Guidelines extrapolate from the IST+CAST window.
- **4% never CT-scanned** — small fraction of randomized patients did not have CT to exclude ICH; 1.5–2% had final diagnosis other than ischaemic stroke (a few haemorrhages and tumours misdiagnosed at randomisation).
- **Pulmonary embolism likely under-reported** — recorded rate (0.8%) much lower than the 3–9% in contemporary reviews; the open-label and retrospective in-hospital reporting probably missed events.
- **No formal placebo for aspirin** — but the parallel placebo-controlled CAST trial supports the IST signal.
- **Pre-thrombolytic era** — IST enrolled 1991–1996. NINDS published December 1995. Most IST patients did not receive alteplase. Modern context: aspirin is delayed 24 h post-alteplase due to bleeding risk; aspirin given immediately if not thrombolysed.
- **Geographic case-mix:** 36 countries, predominantly UK/Europe. Stroke subtypes weighted toward extracranial carotid and cardioembolic; lacunar fraction lower than in Chinese population.

## Heparin caveat (clinically important, often misremembered)

The IST is frequently invoked in arguments for or against heparin in acute stroke. Authors' own conclusion (verbatim, p. 1580):

> "These results provide evidence against the routine use, in patients with acute ischaemic stroke, of any heparin regimen as intensive as the subcutaneous 'medium-dose' regimen, and provide no indication for the selective use of such a regimen even for the immediate treatment of particular types of patient (such as those with cardioembolic or vertebrobasilar ischaemic stroke)."

The 14-day haemorrhagic-stroke excess (+8 per 1000) and the +9 per 1000 transfused/fatal extracranial bleed excess made heparin a net wash at 6 months (death-or-dependence 62.9% in both arms). The IST is the primary RCT-level evidence that **routine therapeutic heparin in acute ischaemic stroke is not indicated** — a position carried forward by AHA/ASA from 1997 to the present.

## Geographic generalizability

- UK + 35 other countries (international): primarily Western/European population, secondarily Australasian. Stroke subtype distribution differs from Chinese populations: CAST's predominantly small-vessel/lacunar and intracranial atherosclerotic case-mix vs IST's broader extracranial-carotid + cardioembolic mix. Both trials produced concordant aspirin signals — the joint analysis is the basis for global generalizability.

## Conclusion (per medical-scientist)

IST established that early aspirin (300 mg/day, within 48 h of acute ischaemic stroke) produces a small but real net benefit on early stroke recurrence and 6-month death or dependency, with a small excess of transfused/fatal extracranial bleeds and minimal additional haemorrhagic-stroke risk. Subcutaneous unfractionated heparin (low or medium dose) at the doses tested showed no net 6-month benefit and increased haemorrhagic stroke and major extracranial bleeding — providing the foundational RCT evidence against routine heparin in acute ischaemic stroke. Together with the parallel CAST trial (n=21,106), IST contributes to a pooled ~40,000-patient evidence base supporting the Class I, Level A AHA/ASA recommendation for aspirin within 24-48 h of acute ischaemic stroke once ICH is excluded.
