Stroke Basics

Last reviewed 2026-02-14 · Reviewed by medical-scientist

What is stroke?

Stroke is defined as a sudden neurological deficit caused by focal brain ischemia or hemorrhage, lasting more than 24 hours or leading to death. Ischemic stroke accounts for approximately 87% of all strokes and results from occlusion of a cerebral artery by thrombus or embolism.

Transient ischemic attacks (TIAs) present identically but resolve within 24 hours with no permanent tissue injury on imaging. However, the risk of stroke within 48–72 hours of TIA is 3–10%, with the ABCD2 score helping to stratify urgency.

Initial assessment

The National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) provides a standardized measure of stroke severity on a 0–42 scale. Scores are used to guide thrombolysis and EVT eligibility decisions. A score of ≥6 typically indicates moderate-to-severe stroke warranting urgent imaging.

Time is brain: approximately 1.9 million neurons are lost every minute during a large vessel occlusion without treatment. The phrase "time is brain" underpins the current emphasis on door-to-needle times under 45 minutes for eligible patients.

Key evidence

The NINDS tPA trial (1995, NEJM) established IV alteplase within 3 hours as the standard of care. Subsequent trials extended this window to 4.5 hours (ECASS-III, 2008) and further with imaging-guided selection (EXTEND, 2019; WAKE-UP, 2018). For large vessel occlusions, five landmark RCTs in 2015 established EVT as the standard of care.
