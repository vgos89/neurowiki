Study Arms — style decision

Trial context: DAWN (Nogueira 2018) · Trevo thrombectomy vs Medical management · Wake-up / unknown-onset ischaemic stroke

Reference

How to interpret this trial (TeachingWell — current production style)

Cobalt left border · slate-50 fill · collapsible · NO eyebrow header — this is the target style for the "all accordions look the same" option.

Proves

Trevo thrombectomy improves 90-day functional independence (mRS 0–2) compared with medical management alone in patients with wake-up or late-window strokes selected by clinical–imaging mismatch.

Does not prove

Benefit extends to patients without mismatch criteria, or that all thrombectomy devices are equivalent. The trial was stopped early (206/500 patients) which may have inflated the effect size.

Cautions

Mismatch selection (NIHSS ≥10, infarct <31 mL on DWI or <1/3 MCA territory) is mandatory — the result does not generalise beyond this criteria. Enrolment stopped early by DSMB after interim analysis.

Design note — trade-off

Option A (keep current) is the stronger default if Study Arms is understood as a structured factual summary — equivalent to Population, Outcome, Trial Design, and Safety — all of which share the white content-section card. The eyebrow "STUDY ARMS" label and consistent card frame signal that Study Arms is peer content to those sections, not a teaching overlay.

Option B (match the wells) is the right call if the priority is visual uniformity of every collapsible accordion on the page. It removes the only card that mixes a section-card outer shell with an accordion interior, giving the page a single collapsible language. The cost is that Study Arms no longer visually clusters with Population / Outcome / Safety, which could leave a clinician unsure whether it is editorial content or factual trial data.

Suggested default: Option A — preserve the content-section card for factual trial data; reserve the cobalt-well pattern exclusively for teaching annotations (How to read, How to interpret). This sharpens the semantic contrast between "what the trial showed" (card) and "how to think about it" (well). If the page later gains more collapsible teaching wells, reconsider Option B.
